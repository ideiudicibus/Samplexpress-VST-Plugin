The Three Playback Modes
The core workflow of Simpler revolves around its three distinct playback modes, which completely alter how the dropped sample behaves across your MIDI keyboard:

1. Classic Mode
How it works: This is the traditional sampler behavior. When you press a key, the sample plays back polyphonically (allowing chords).

Pitching: Playing a higher note speeds up and raises the pitch of the sample; playing a lower note slows it down and lowers the pitch.

Best for: Creating custom synth pads, bass instruments, or plucked sounds out of a single audio snapshot.

2. One-Shot Mode
How it works: Designed specifically for monophonic playback (one note at a time). Unlike Classic mode, hitting a key triggers the entire sample from start to finish (or up to your designated loop/fade points) even if you release the key immediately.

Warping integration: It utilizes Ableton’s Warp engine. This means if you change your project tempo, the sample can stay perfectly in time without changing its pitch.

Best for: Drum hits (kicks, snares, claps) and sound effects.

3. Slice Mode
How it works: Simpler analyzes the transient spikes in the audio file and automatically chops the sample into fragments, mapping each slice to a consecutive MIDI note (starting at C1 by default).

Slicing types: You can slice by Transient (automatic detection), Beat (fixed rhythmic grid like 1/8 or 1/16 notes), Region (equal subdivisions), or Manual (double-clicking to add your own markers).

Best for: Chopping up drum breaks, rearranging vocal loops, or remixing melodic phrases.

Key Synthesis and Modulation Controls
Simpler embeds a miniature subtractive synthesizer engine directly below the waveform display to help shape your sound:

Warp Controls: When enabled, you can choose between Ableton's standard warping algorithms (Complex, Pro, Tones, Texture, Beats). This lets you stretch or compress a sample's length independently of its pitch.

Filter Section: Features a multi-mode analog-modeled filter (Clean, OSR, MS2, SMP, PRD) inspired by classic hardware synths. It includes controls for Frequency, Resonance, and dedicated Drive to add warmth or grit.

Envelopes: You get three distinct ADSR (Attack, Decay, Sustain, Release) envelope controls to shape the Volume (Amplitude), Filter cutoff, and Pitch over time.

LFO (Low Frequency Oscillator): A simple modulation source that can be routed to modulate the Filter Frequency, Pitch, Volume, or Pan to create movement, vibrato, or tremolo effects.

Pro Tip: If you ever find yourself hitting the creative ceiling of a single sample and need multi-sampling zones (e.g., mapping different samples to different key ranges or velocities), you can simply right-click Simpler’s title bar and select "Simpler -> Sampler" to instantly convert it without losing your settings.
